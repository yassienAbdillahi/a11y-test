Hello, my name is Sarah, and I live with COPD.

I was diagnosed about seven years ago after struggling with breathlessness for quite a long time. At first, I thought I was simply getting older and becoming less fit, but eventually I reached a point where even everyday activities like climbing the stairs or carrying shopping became difficult.

When I was first diagnosed, I felt overwhelmed. There was a lot of information to take in, and I wasn't sure what the future would look like. I also felt quite isolated because none of my friends or family members had experienced the condition themselves.

A few months later, my respiratory nurse told me about a local support group run with the help of the National Respiratory Support Alliance. I was nervous about attending at first, but it turned out to be one of the best decisions I've made.

The group gave me the opportunity to meet other people living with COPD and other respiratory conditions. Hearing their experiences helped me understand that I wasn't alone and that many of the challenges I was facing were shared by others.

The support group also introduced me to practical techniques for managing my symptoms, including breathing exercises and ways to plan activities so that I could conserve energy. I learned more about pulmonary rehabilitation and found out about services that I didn't even know existed.

What made the biggest difference was the confidence I gained. Before joining the group, I often avoided social activities because I worried about becoming short of breath. Over time, I became more comfortable talking about my condition and asking for support when I needed it.

Today, I still have difficult days, but I feel much better equipped to manage my COPD. The information, encouragement and sense of community I've received have had a real impact on my quality of life.

If you've recently been diagnosed with a respiratory condition, my advice would be to reach out for support. Connecting with people who understand what you're going through can make a huge difference.

Thank you for listening.
